# Dr. Anna Caraveli — Book Interview for "The Unlikely Bits"

**Interviewer:** Curtis Michelson
**Date:** April 2026
**Context:** Interview for Curtis's book *The Unlikely Bits: How The Best Designers, Disruptors and Detectives Leverage Clues Others Miss*

## Topics Covered
- The role of outsiders in our investigations
- Why some notice details that others don't
- The virtues of ambiguity and cognitive flexibility
- Avoiding false closure
- Asking profoundly better questions
- Leveraging blind spots
- The primacy of circles, revisiting old clues with fresh eyes

## Life Backdrop

Anna was a rebellious person, left Greece at 18, stayed in Paris France a while. Wound up teaching, studying at University of Texas, El Paso. Married a San Francisco rock musician.

Focused on a learning path, not a career. Her grad work was comparative literature — loved the aesthetics, but she was missing the people.

Went then to the ancient epics — oral traditions, "teller of tales." Homer, Iliad, communities, troubadours — who created the stories. That changed her perspective.

She rediscovered the oral tradition alive today in Greece. Folk songs... laments for the dead, living traditions. She did fieldwork in villages, studied language in human context. The very act of singing creates a community of mourners. Singing and performing in a community — adapting, improvising. People and creation.

She taught some but didn't land in academia.

> "I put together pieces of my life and study that were self evident, but others didn't see how they fit together." —Dr. Anna Caraveli

## Smithsonian & The Demand Perspective

Got hired by Smithsonian in Washington DC. Ran courses there, started sensing the demand perspective. Created a concept — "Campus on the Mall" — it doubled enrollment. Helped them see a new identity, a new path, beyond being a museum.

Built partnerships and collaboratives with others in DC. Even drew plans for a "Virtual Smithsonian" — brought TV networks in — but couldn't make it happen. (This was in the 90s — Anna has always been ahead.) The Smithsonian finally did do a virtual thing, but they were too late to that game.

## Brookings & Executive Education

Moved on to Brookings think tank — not a good fit, too much policy. But learned about corporate partnerships. Worked in executive education for them.

Asked the deeper questions: "How are we helping execs really solve their deepest problems?"

She saw other think tanks charging 10x more for their services than Brookings. She would ask end-customers "What keeps you up at night?" — not "Which programs can we improve?"

> "I learned that people LOVE these kinds of open-ended questions — 'Tell me about your business,' 'What is your vision for this school/industry?' She found out, nobody ever asks them these."

She believes in getting to what matters, what touches the heart.

## On Capability: Born or Learned?

Anna referenced the work of Michael Maccoby — the concept of the "constructive narcissist."

The best, most innovative thinkers or disruptors usually have some way to put a foot in the material world (solution space) and they are folks who live in problem/opportunity space most of the time.

Finding and keeping good collaboration partners can be a gap. She said: "I never found the right partner, it limited me."

## On Trusting the Unusual Path

When a client asked for a fix or a solution, it was self-evident to Anna that their question is overly constrained.

She is (and has always been) people first. Go to customers and then work your way back.

Why? Because even if your little local chapter of your large global nonprofit only has 4 attending members — what if those 4 attendees are executives with links, door openers, keepers of precious knowledge and insight. Her interviews always opened up these possibilities.

She believes in learning as you go. And she realized: people want to be part of the design — co-creative. Big strategy docs is not the way. A learning approach is key.

## Key Themes for The Unlikely Bits

- **The question behind the question**: Anna's superpower is recognizing that the stated question is overly constrained
- **People first, always**: Go to customers, work backward. The answers are in the field, not the boardroom.
- **Co-creation over prescription**: People want to be part of the design
- **Learning path over career path**: Her unusual trajectory (Greece → Paris → Texas → oral traditions → Smithsonian → Brookings → The Demand Networks) was guided by curiosity, not career planning
- **Oral tradition as methodology**: The fieldwork studying Greek laments — community, improvisation, adaptation — became her consulting methodology
- **Constructive narcissism (Maccoby)**: The ability to live in problem space while keeping one foot in solution space
- **Ahead of the curve**: Virtual Smithsonian in the 90s, Campus on the Mall, demand perspective before it was mainstream
