## Part 1: Marketing Strategy & Positioning

### Brand Positioning

JOTF's positioning is unique: innovation games and strategic facilitation powered by a compounding AI backend. When advising on marketing:

- **Know the moat**: The moat isn't the games or tools — it's the compounding backend (InnoGraph + Signals + methodology). Everything else is a storefront window.
- **Know the audience**: Innovation leaders, strategy consultants, C-suite in mid-market companies ($50M-500M revenue) looking for facilitation that's more than post-its and whiteboards.
- **Know the competitors**: Mural, LUMA Institute, Strategyzer, Liberating Structures — none have the AI-powered graph backend.

### Campaign Planning

When asked to plan a campaign or promotion:

1. **Goal** — What are we trying to achieve? (Awareness, leads, activation, retention)
2. **Audience** — Who specifically? (Role, company size, pain point, where they hang out)
3. **Message** — What's the one thing we want them to believe after seeing this?
4. **Channel** — Where will this reach them? (LinkedIn, email, event, partner, content)
5. **Proof** — What evidence backs our claim? (Case study, demo, testimonial, data)
6. **CTA** — What's the one next step? (Book a call, try the game, read the report)

### Content Marketing

Content should serve a purpose in the funnel:

| Stage | Content Type | Goal |
|-------|-------------|------|
| **Awareness** | Collision briefs, Signal reports, provocative takes | Get discovered, build authority |
| **Interest** | Case studies, demo videos, methodology articles | Show what's different |
| **Consideration** | Game walkthroughs, ROI frameworks, comparison pages | Convince it works |
| **Decision** | Testimonials, pilot proposals, pricing guides | Remove friction |

### Messaging Frameworks

**Value Prop Formula**: For [audience] who [pain point], JOTF is the [category] that [key benefit] because [reason to believe].

**Before/After/Bridge**: What the world looks like before JOTF → what it looks like after → how we get them there.

**Problem-Agitate-Solve**: Name the problem → make them feel it → present the solution.

### Promotional Copy

When writing promotional copy (emails, social posts, landing pages):
- Lead with the insight, not the product
- Specific > vague ("3 strategic moves in 45 minutes" > "innovative workshop")
- One CTA per piece — don't split attention
- Show the transformation, not the feature
- Use the customer's language, not ours

### Email Marketing

**Teaser/announcement email pattern:**
```
Subject: [Specific, curiosity-driven — what they'll get]

Body:
- 1-2 sentence hook (the insight or finding)
- 1 sentence context (why it matters now)
- Clear CTA link with descriptive text
- Brief, personal sign-off
```

**Newsletter/brief pattern:**
- Lead with the most surprising finding
- 3-5 items max — curated, not comprehensive
- Each item: headline + 2-sentence take + link
- Consistent cadence matters more than length

### Social Media

**LinkedIn** (primary B2B channel for JOTF):
- Posts that teach > posts that sell
- Open with a hook line (question, counterintuitive stat, bold claim)
- Short paragraphs, use line breaks generously
- End with a question or invitation to comment
- Tag people and companies when relevant
- Carousels and documents get higher reach than text-only

**General social principles:**
- 80% value, 20% promotion
- Repurpose: every collision brief = 3-5 social posts
- Show the work: behind-the-scenes of game sessions, graph visualizations, real results
- Engage with commenters — algorithms reward conversation

## Part 2: GEO (Generative Engine Optimization)

GEO is one weapon in the arsenal — making content discoverable and citable by AI systems (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Gemini, Google AI Overviews). AI engines have no paid ranking. A small brand can outrank a $100M company if the content is better structured and more citable.

### SEO vs GEO

| Dimension | SEO (Traditional) | GEO (AI Search) |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------|
| Target | Google's algorithm | AI language models |
| Unit | Keywords (1-3 words) | Prompts (5-15+ word questions) |
| Goal | Rank on page 1 | Get **cited** as a source |
| Signal | Backlinks, keywords, domain authority | Structure, citability, authority, freshness |
| Opportunity | Competitive, pay-to-play | Small brands can outrank large ones |

**Critical stats:**
- AI Overviews appear in ~45% of Google searches
- AI Overviews reduce website clicks by up to 58%
- Brands are 6.5x more likely to be cited via third-party sources than their own domains
- Adding statistics to passages increases citation by 40%
- Adding quotations from authorities increases citation by up to 115%
- Optimized content gets cited 3x more often than non-optimized
- Lower-ranked sites (4th-5th position) benefit most from GEO — up to 115% visibility lift

### The Three Pillars of AI Visibility

1. **Structure** — Content formatted for extraction (answer blocks, self-contained passages, tables, schema markup)
2. **Authority** — Trusted source signals (third-party mentions, citations, credentials, original data)
3. **Presence** — AI crawlers can access and index content (robots.txt, meta tags, llms.txt)

## Part 3: AI Citability Scoring (0-100)

Use this rubric when auditing any page or content piece. Score each category, then calculate the weighted total.

### Category 1: Answer Block Quality (30%)

Does content contain clear, quotable passages AI can extract verbatim?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 90-100 | Every section opens with 1-2 sentence direct answer. "X is..." patterns. First 40-60 words stand alone. |
| 70-89 | Most sections have clear answer openings. Some definition patterns. |
| 50-69 | Some answer-like openings but many bury the answer mid-paragraph. |
| 30-49 | Answers generally buried in long paragraphs. Narrative-driven. |
| 0-29 | No identifiable answer blocks. AI would struggle to extract anything. |

**High-citability example:**
> Content delivery networks (CDNs) are distributed server systems that cache and serve web content from locations geographically close to end users. A CDN reduces latency by 50-70% on average by serving assets from edge servers rather than a single origin server.

**Low-citability example:**
> If you've ever wondered why some websites load faster than others, the answer might surprise you. There's this amazing technology that has been around for a while now...

### Category 2: Passage Self-Containment (25%)

Can individual passages be understood without surrounding context?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 90-100 | 80%+ blocks self-contained. Names subject explicitly. No dangling pronouns. |
| 70-89 | 60-79% self-contained. Most name their subject. |
| 50-69 | 40-59% self-contained. Mixed explicit subjects and pronouns. |
| 30-49 | Heavy reliance on "it," "this," "they" referencing earlier content. |
| 0-29 | Continuous narrative — extracting any paragraph loses meaning. |

**Self-containment checklist:**
1. Does the passage name the subject (not "it," "this," "they")?
2. Can someone understand the main point reading ONLY this passage?
3. Does it contain at least one specific fact, statistic, or named entity?
4. Is it 50-200 words? (Optimal AI extraction length: **134-167 words**)
5. Does it avoid starting with conjunctions ("But," "However") implying prior context?

### Category 3: Structural Readability (20%)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 90-100 | Clean H1>H2>H3 hierarchy. Question-based headings. Short paragraphs (2-4 sentences). Tables for comparisons. Lists for processes/features. |
| 50-69 | Hierarchy present but inconsistent. Few question headings. Mixed paragraph lengths. |
| 0-29 | No heading structure. Wall-of-text paragraphs. No tables or lists. |

### Category 4: Statistical Density (15%)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 90-100 | 5+ specific statistics per 500 words. All claims backed by named sources or dates. Exact numbers, not "many" or "several." |
| 50-69 | 1-2 stats per 500 words. Some sourced claims. Mix of specific and vague. |
| 0-29 | No statistics. No sourced claims. Vague quantifiers throughout. |

**Counts as a statistic:** "73% of marketers report...", "$4,500 per month", "6-8 weeks", "2025 HubSpot report"
**Does NOT count:** "Many companies use...", "Studies show...", "Experts agree..."

### Category 5: Uniqueness & Original Data (10%)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 90-100 | First-party research, proprietary data, original surveys, unique datasets. |
| 50-69 | Synthesizes existing info with some unique commentary or examples. |
| 0-29 | Entirely derivative. All info available on higher-authority sources. |

### Scoring Formula

**Page Citability Score** = (Answer × 0.30) + (SelfContain × 0.25) + (Structure × 0.20) + (Stats × 0.15) + (Unique × 0.10)

### AI System Citation Preferences

| AI System | What It Prefers |
|-----------|----------------|
| **ChatGPT Search** | Explicit definitions, named sources, recent dates. Cites 2-4 sources/response. |
| **Perplexity** | Fact-dense passages with statistics. Cites 4-8 sources. Values recency highly. |
| **Claude** | Well-structured, comprehensive passages. Values nuance and accuracy. |
| **Gemini/AI Overviews** | Concise answer blocks (40-60 words). Values top-10 organic ranking. |
| **Copilot (Bing)** | High-authority domains with clear factual claims. |

## Part 5: GEO Prompt Research

### The 8 Query Types People Ask AI

When researching what prompts to target, organize into these buckets:

1. **Definition** — "What is [topic]?" / "How does [X] work?"
2. **Recommendation** — "What is the best [X] for [use case]?" / "Top [X] in [year]"
3. **Comparison** — "[X] vs [Y]" / "[X] alternatives"
4. **Evaluation** — "Is [X] worth it?" / "Pros and cons of [X]"
5. **How-to** — "How to [solve problem]" / "Step-by-step guide to [task]"
6. **Cost** — "How much does [X] cost?" / "[X] pricing"
7. **Landscape** — "What are the types of [X]?" / "[X] industry overview"
8. **Use case** — "Can [X] be used for [Y]?" / "[X] for [specific industry]"

### Prompt Prioritization Tiers

| Tier | Label | Description | Priority |
|------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | **Buy** | Direct purchase/sales intent prompts | Highest |
| 2 | **Solve** | Methodology, solutions, how-to prompts | High |
| 3 | **Learn** | Authority, education, thought leadership | Medium |

**Key insight:** Lower-ranked sites benefit most from GEO. If you're not #1 on Google, GEO is your best path to visibility.

## Part 6: Content Optimization Techniques

### Answer-First Writing

Every section should open with a direct answer in the first 1-2 sentences, then expand. The reader (and AI) should get the core point immediately.

**Pattern:** "[Subject] is [definition/answer]. [Supporting detail with specific data.]"

### Optimal Passage Length

The sweet spot for AI-citable passages is **134-167 words**. Each passage should be:
- Self-contained (understandable in isolation)
- Fact-rich (statistics, dates, named entities)
- Answer-first (lead with the point)

### GEO Content Boosters (Research-Backed)

| Technique | Visibility Lift | How to Apply |
|-----------|----------------|--------------|
| Add quotations from authorities | +115% (max) | Quote named experts, researchers, practitioners |
| Add statistics with sources | +40% | Cite specific numbers from named studies |
| Add source citations | +20-25% | "According to [Source Name] ([Year])..." |
| Fluency optimization | +30% | Clean, professional prose (no AI clichés) |
| Definition patterns | +2.1x citation rate | "X is [definition]" in first sentence |

### De-AI-ify Checklist

Before publishing, strip these AI-generated patterns:
- Overused transitions: "Furthermore," "Moreover," "It's worth noting that"
- AI clichés: "In today's rapidly evolving landscape," "game-changer," "revolutionize"
- Hedging: "It's important to note that," "It should be mentioned that"
- Corporate buzzwords: "leverage," "synergy," "cutting-edge," "state-of-the-art"
- Robotic patterns: Starting every paragraph with "The," numbered lists for everything

**Rule:** If it sounds like something an AI would write, rewrite it. Authentic voice > polished AI prose.

## Part 7: Copywriting Principles

### Clarity Over Cleverness

- Lead with the benefit, not the feature
- One idea per sentence, one point per paragraph
- Specific > vague ("saves 3 hours/week" > "saves time")
- Active voice > passive voice

### Page-Specific Guidance

**Homepage:** Hero section answers "What is this?" in 6 seconds. Value prop + who it's for + proof point.

**Landing pages:** One CTA per page. Problem → Solution → Proof → Action.

**About page:** Credentials, methodology, origin story. This is where E-E-A-T signals live.

**Blog/articles:** Question-based headings. Answer-first paragraphs. Statistics and sources throughout. 134-167 word self-contained passages.

### Teaser Email Pattern

When sending promotional emails via `smtp-send`:

```
Subject: [Specific, curiosity-driven — what they'll get]

Body:
- 1-2 sentence hook (the insight or finding)
- 1 sentence context (why it matters now)
- Clear CTA link with descriptive text
- Signed: Jedi Claw / jedionthefly.com
```

Keep it short. The email is the teaser, not the content.

1. **Research** — Discover prompts people ask AI about our topics (Wardley Maps, innovation, strategy)
